00 Cover · Applied AI · The middle band

Frontier ships as papers.
Consumer ships as demos.
Applied AI ships as systems.

The AI market splits three ways in 2026: research that proves a capability, consumer products that dramatise it, and enterprise systems that have to survive scale, tenancy, audit, schema change, and adversarial input. Applied AI is the middle band — the discipline of taking a novel research result or a consumer-grade feature and converting it into an enterprise substrate with measured properties, named failure modes, and an adoption path. This entry closes the gap on one capability: long-term memory for coding-orchestration agents.

01 Executive summary

Three findings, one decision.

Frontier labs publish capability jumps. Consumer products convert a subset into compelling features. Neither layer solves the enterprise problem: research rarely ships with tenancy, observability, or operating guarantees; consumer features optimise for one user, not for organisational load. Applied AI is the work between them. This entry closes the gap on one capability — long-term memory for coding-orchestration agents.

Finding 1 — Production-weighted retrieval

The chunk-text retrieval approach outperformed the entity-graph approach by 36 percentage points when the test was weighted to match real production query frequency. Under naïve uniform weighting the two looked tied; the weighting decided the outcome.

Finding 2 — Fabrication count

Zero versus four out of 128 paired trials. The substrate that returns literal source text never invented content; the substrate that runs an extraction step occasionally surfaced a confidently-wrong supersession claim.

Finding 3 — Stale-fact inversion by executor tier

Bi-temporal metadata helped a smaller executor and hurt a more-capable one in the pilot signal. The stronger executor sometimes re-quoted the older fact because the metadata told it the older fact existed. Context design must match executor capability; it is not a universal default until the formal suite is re-run.

For delivery teams: default to chunk-text retrieval for SDLC-shaped corpora, reserve graph-summary substrates for narrow entity-disambiguation, gate any bi-temporal context on the executor's capability tier, and reject adoption candidates against named criteria rather than taste. Sections 09 and 10 carry the platform and delivery implications.

Scope: not exhaustive vendor coverage. The agent-memory / RAG-substrate space in 2026 includes Letta, MemGPT, Cognee, Zep, Pinecone Assistants, LangMem, Mastra, and others. The evaluation covers four candidates on one delivery stack: LightRAG, Graphiti, Mem0, and Tencent DB Agent-Memory. The retrieval canary measured LightRAG, Graphiti, and a filesystem baseline. Mem0 was screened as redundant with Graphiti (see §04). Tencent DB Agent-Memory did not clear the adoption bar (see §08).

One data point in a broader landscape — your canary on your corpus is still the deciding test. The pattern transfers across vendors and classes; the specific magnitudes here do not transfer without re-measurement on your stack.

02 Why memory matters · Two enterprise classes

Two corpora, one retrieval problem.

Across EPAM accounts the memory-substrate question recurs because agents are useful only when they retrieve the right knowledge at the right moment. The corpus shape varies; the underlying problem does not. Two classes cover the majority of the work.

A SDLC corpus

The project's own past, queried by its present.

Architecture decisions, tickets, source-control heritage — ADRs, design records, ticket history, post-incident reviews, runbooks, architecture invariants. The project's own past, queried by the people working on its present.

B Business / domain corpus

Customer-specific knowledge with high retention pressure — case files (legal, medical, insurance, support), regulatory references, product knowledge, customer-account history. High volume, high query specificity, high cost of a wrong answer that gets acted on.

The shared problem

Both classes share the same underlying retrieval problem: fetch the relevant knowledge at maximum retrieval efficiency, with bounded hallucination, when the agent needs it. The substrate-selection question is identical; only the corpus shape and the production query distribution differ. A finding about substrate behavior on one class transfers to the other with the caveat that the production query distribution is different - SDLC is canonical-current-text-heavy; business / domain is entity-disambiguation-heavy.

For delivery teams: identify which of the two classes your customer's corpus belongs to before choosing a substrate. Defaulting to the vendor's recommendation without establishing the corpus class is the most common reason a substrate that looked promising in evaluation underperforms in production.

This report's specific work was carried out on an SDLC-shaped corpus of around 130 architecture-decision and investigation documents. The shape numbers below are illustrative of an SDLC corpus of that scale; the pattern that follows generalises across either class with the noted caveat on query distribution.

Why memory is the canonical example of the frontier-to-enterprise gap

The plain-language version

Two of the biggest consumer-AI products on the market ship a feature called "memory". Both vendors mean different things by the word. Neither shipped definition resembles the contract an enterprise architect would write for memory infrastructure. That mismatch is what an Applied AI report on memory has to sit on top of.

Consumer "memory" is the cleanest illustration of why the middle band exists. Two reverse-engineering writeups (Embrace The Red, 2025; Shlok Khemani, 2025) establish that ChatGPT's memory feature is not a vector database, not entity-resolved, and not a graph. It is a bulleted text dossier appended to the system prompt; the schema is whatever the model wrote last time; the dossier survives no model upgrade with structural guarantees; and it has been shown poisonable via prompt injection (arXiv 2406.00199). Anthropic's Claude memory ships a different architecture entirely -a filesystem the model manipulates with explicit tools -more transparent than ChatGPT's dossier, but still per-user, locked to one API surface, and bounded by the live context window (~150-200 reliable instruction slots before retrieval-rot).

Both surfaces are reasonable products for the consumer use case (personalising a single person's chat experience). Neither answers the enterprise checklist a memory substrate would have to clear before adoption: multi-tenant isolation, decision-trace logging for SOC2 / ISO 27001, schema evolution under governance, PII sanitization, per-tenant partitioning, recovery after a model upgrade, team-shared layer with role-based access. The Atlan enterprise memory checklist and the Vendia enterprise-memory writeup spell out the contract surface; the consumer features ship zero of it.

The only public quantitative comparison between a consumer memory feature and a purpose-built memory substrate - Mem0 vs OpenAI native memory, 2025 -reports a 26 percentage-point accuracy gap in Mem0's favour on LoCoMo with 91% lower p95 latency. Vendor-authored, read with the appropriate discount; the methodology is public and the gap is the largest reproducible measurement of the consumer-vs-purpose-built delta currently in the open literature.

What this section is not arguing. It is not arguing that consumer-AI memory is badly designed. The architecture choices both vendors made are coherent for the audience the products serve. The argument is narrower: the word memory in consumer-AI marketing names a user-experience feature, not a contract. Enterprise memory needs a contract. That gap is the work this report belongs to. The substrate comparison in §04 onwards is one specific cut at one specific slice of that work: long-term semantic memory for SDLC coding-orchestration agents.

03 The adoption bar · What "good" looks like in production

Five criteria, stated before any candidate was scored.

A substrate that survives the first production query is one we selected against named criteria, not against taste. Stating the criteria first is what makes a rejection reproducible by another team.

The five-criterion adoption bar used throughout this report:

CriterionRequired bar
Integration surface Python-callable read path, MCP-compatible agent interface, multi-tenant per-workspace mode.
Install hygiene Installing leaves third-party software unchanged. No host-environment patches, no silent fall-through.
Test coverage Non-trivial test suite exercising the consuming-stack API. A wired-but-empty harness fails this.
Maintenance posture More than one active contributor, sustainable release cadence, API stabilised over time.
Operational fit Routes through EPAM DIAL, telemetry to existing observability stack, secrets via managed store.

For delivery teams: write your adoption bar before you evaluate candidates. A bar applied post-hoc rationalises a decision already made; a bar applied a priori constrains the decision and is reproducible by the next reviewer.

The honest framing

The five criteria above are not universal. They are calibrated to one specific delivery context (Python-first stack, EPAM DIAL gateway, MCP-compatible agent surface). A team with a TypeScript-only stack would have a different "integration surface" criterion; a team with a different gateway would have a different "operational fit" criterion. The discipline is to state the bar; the contents of the bar are domain-specific.

04 Test design · Substrates, tasks, judging

Three substrates, eight task families, two judges in opposite orderings.

The empirical design served one question: which substrate would perform best on the SDLC corpus's actual production query distribution, evaluated by two independent judges in a way that rules out the most common position-bias failure.

Substrates compared

Three approaches that span the practical design space for enterprise memory retrieval, each tested using a specific implementation:

  • Chunk-text retrieval, tested as LightRAG (HKU, arXiv:2410.05779). Documents are split into chunks; an embedding index plus a parallel keyword index returns the literal source text for a query. The unit returned is the chunk, with the source-document path attached. Mature, low-overhead, predictable.
  • Entity-graph retrieval with bi-temporal metadata, tested as Graphiti (Zep, arXiv:2501.13956). An extraction step pulls entities and relations from each ingest; nodes carry valid-from and superseded-on timestamps; queries return node summaries plus the relations between them. The unit returned is a small, structured slice of the graph.
  • Filesystem baseline - the trust-of-last-resort. Keyword extraction from the query, then a recursive grep over the corpus, then the first matched files' content sent to the judge. This is the fallback path an engineer takes when no purpose-built substrate is available.

Why Mem0 was considered and excluded

Mem0 was the first candidate considered, with marketing posture closest to Graphiti's. The Mem0 paper and the Zep rebuttal blog dispute each other on LoCoMo by roughly 10-30 percentage points depending on whose methodology is used. Mem0 is structurally the same approach as Graphiti: an extraction-mediated semantic store with episodic memory and an LLM-rewrite step at ingest. Running Mem0 as a separate head-to-head against Graphiti would yield a second sample of the same approach, not a comparison of different approaches. Where Graphiti landed in this report's findings is roughly where Mem0 would land given the same retrieval mechanism.

For a delivery team currently running on Mem0, the findings about Graphiti below probably transfer with the caveat that Mem0's retrieval API surface, schema discipline, and operational properties differ in detail. If the question is "should you run Mem0 instead of Graphiti", a separate head-to-head between those two on your corpus would be the right experiment; this report does not answer it.

Where this report sits in the public landscape

Prior public work on agent-memory substrate comparison includes the Mem0 paper above, the Zep / Graphiti paper above, the unbiased-evaluation GraphRAG paper (whose position / length / trial bias controls motivated parts of the judging design below), and Cognee's AI Memory Benchmarking blog post (the only prior public prior public artefact that puts Cognee, LightRAG, Graphiti, and Mem0 head-to-head on a shared corpus - with the caveat that Cognee re-used Graphiti's numbers from a smaller study rather than re-running Graphiti).

To the best of public-source review, this is the first non-vendor head-to-head comparison of LightRAG and Graphiti on a single corpus under a substrate-fair fixture. This report does not arbitrate the Mem0 vs Graphiti / Zep cross-fire; the two vendors' published numbers are irreconcilable from public artefacts, and a third fair-fixture re-run on LoCoMo is out of scope here. If counter-examples to "first non-vendor LightRAG vs Graphiti" exist, the report is happy to be the second.

Why these task families

The test set was eight task families chosen because they cover the operations a coding-orchestration agent actually performs on an SDLC corpus. The two enterprise classes from Section 02 each have a dominant query type; the test families intersect both:

How the judging worked

What "position-swap judging" means (one sentence)

The same question is shown to each judge twice - with the two candidate retrievals in opposite orderings; a pair only "wins" when the judge prefers the same candidate in both orderings. This rules out a class of bias where the judge prefers whichever retrieval is shown first.

Sixty-four paired questions ran through every pair of substrates (chunk-text vs filesystem, entity-graph vs filesystem, chunk-text vs entity-graph). Each retrieval was scored by two independent judges in both orderings. During this evaluation window the judging calls ran on the bench harness's interim provider path, not on the EPAM DIAL gateway that production agents are intended to use; a DIAL-canonical re-run is an open follow-up before treating the retrieval findings as fully transferable to DIAL-routed deployments. The two judges were chosen from different model families so that family-specific preferences would surface as judge disagreement rather than as a silent bias.

Two judges in opposite orderings is more conservative than single-judge / single-ordering setups: position-swap collapse discards information when the two orderings disagree, which inflates the tie rate but rules out the position-bias artefact that has surfaced in published LLM-judge benchmark critiques. The rule-change threshold was set in advance: a result clears the bar at a ≥20 percentage-point absolute gap, a ≥2× difference in fabrication rate, or a confidence interval that excludes zero.

For delivery teams: when you run your own canary, do not skip the position-swap. The over-counting of ties is a feature; it forces the headline result to survive judge order, which is the only headline worth carrying into a deployment decision.

05 Headline result · The weighting is the finding

Naïve weighting said tie. Production weighting said 36 points.

The retrieval-quality comparison between the two purpose-built substrates went in opposite directions depending on how the eight task families were combined into a single aggregate number. Which one is right is a question about the production workload, not about statistics.

For delivery teams: when a vendor reports a single aggregate number across multiple task families, ask which weighting they used. A vendor's aggregate that gives every family equal weight is not the aggregate that maps to your production. The discrepancy between the two weightings is often larger than the difference between the substrates themselves.

What "uniform vs frequency-weighted aggregate" means (one sentence)

A uniform-weighted aggregate counts every test family equally; a frequency-weighted aggregate counts each family by how often it actually shows up in real production queries. The two often disagree on which substrate wins - that disagreement is usually the finding.

The two numbers

Under uniform weighting (each of the eight task families given equal mass), the chunk-text and entity-graph substrates were statistically tied: a small win-rate gap with a confidence interval that crossed zero. That is the number a benchmark report that stopped at the uniform aggregate would publish.

Re-weighted to the production query distribution from Section 04 (current canonical text at ~50%, handoff continuity at ~20%, entity disambiguation at ~10%, the remaining five families at ~4% each), the chunk-text substrate beat the entity-graph substrate by roughly 36 percentage points. The two task families that carry 70% of production weight (current canonical text and handoff continuity) are the two where chunk-text wins decisively; the family where the entity-graph substrate wins (entity disambiguation) carries 10% of the weight.

What the same data does not say

Caveats

The 36-point gap is for an SDLC-shaped corpus with the production query distribution stated above. A corpus where entity disambiguation dominates (a customer-support knowledge base where the same product is named differently across regions, for instance) would re-weight in favour of the entity-graph approach. The pattern transfers ("weight by your own production distribution, not by uniform"); the magnitude does not transfer without re-measurement.

The likely mechanism: the chunk-text substrate returns the literal source sentence, which is what "current canonical text" queries actually want. The entity-graph substrate runs an LLM extraction step at ingest that rewrites node summaries, so the literal phrasing is lost by the time the agent retrieves it. For families that need a literal quote, the substrate that never paraphrases wins; for families that need a resolved entity, the substrate that explicitly merges aliases wins.

For delivery teams: when the corpus is SDLC-history-shaped, default to a chunk-text retrieval approach. Reserve graph-summary substrates for the narrow set of queries that need cross-document entity resolution. Don't deploy a single substrate as a universal default; pair the substrate to the dominant query family.

The pre-measurement prior was wrong

The pre-measurement prior favoured Graphiti as the canonical substrate, citing its bi-temporal differentiation and the published vendor numbers in §04. The frequency-weighted measurement above reversed the call. An adoption bar that catches errors before they ship is worth more than an adoption decision that was right on the first try. A delivery team that read the same vendor numbers and skipped the canary would have shipped the slower substrate by default.

06 Reliability · The shape of the failure mode matters

Zero versus four out of 128. Small in absolute, structural in shape.

Counting fabricated answers is not the most powerful retrieval- quality discriminator at this sample size. The shape of the failure mode behind the count is the part that should change a deployment decision.

For delivery teams: restrict autonomous-writeback paths (the agent drafts a decision document, files a ticket, generates a handoff) to the substrate whose failure mode is "the wrong source chunk" rather than the substrate whose failure mode is "an invented relation". The first is recoverable on read; the second is not.

What "hallucination" means here (one sentence)

A retrieval is hallucinated when it returns content that does not exist in any source document; a retrieval is incorrect when it returns the wrong source document; the distinction matters because an incorrect-but-real source can be cross-checked, and a fabricated source cannot.

The two counts

Across 128 paired appearances per substrate, the chunk-text substrate produced zero hallucinated retrievals. The entity-graph substrate produced four (about 3% of its appearances). The filesystem baseline produced zero (its failure mode is "the wrong file head", an incorrect-but-real category, not a fabrication category).

Four out of 128 is too small to clear the rule-change threshold as a statistical claim on its own. What it does cleanly is name the structural difference between the two purpose-built substrates. An LLM extraction step at ingest can occasionally synthesise a relation that no source document records; a chunk-text retrieval pipeline has no extraction step on the read path, so the source text either exists or it does not.

The likely mechanism: all four of the entity-graph substrate's hallucinated outputs were on temporal-supersession queries - exactly the family where the bi-temporal metadata was expected to help. The extraction step occasionally produces a "fact F1 was superseded by fact F2 at time t" relation when no source document records the supersession. The metadata that is supposed to be the differentiator is the surface area where the failure mode lives.

What this finding does not say

It does not say the entity-graph substrate is unsafe at scale. Four out of 128 cannot be generalised that strongly. It says: under autonomous-writeback paths where a single fabricated relation gets acted on, the substrate whose failure mode is "the wrong chunk" has a safer profile than the substrate whose failure mode is "an invented relation". For review-gated assist paths (with explicit approval before any write), the gap is much smaller.

07 Tier interaction · Context design must match executor capability

The same extra context helps a smaller executor and hurts a more-capable one.

The structural finding from the closed-loop pilot - the part that does not appear in any vendor's published numbers - is that the value of extra structural context (the bi-temporal "valid-from / superseded-on" metadata) appeared to flip sign across two executor capability tiers tested on the same workload.

Pilot data, not the formal closed-loop suite

The closed-loop stale-fact counts in this section come from a pilot run (eight tasks per cell, three conditions). The formal closed-loop 8-task suite is deferred; these numbers are directional input to the reversal decision, not formal benchmark results. Treat the per-cell counts as pilot-level only.

For platform teams setting defaults: do not enable bi-temporal context as a global default. Gate it on the executor's capability tier. A smaller / cheaper executor benefits from the explicit supersession metadata; a more-capable executor sometimes re-quotes the older fact because the metadata told it the older fact existed at all. Context design must match executor capability.

What "stale-fact inversion" means (one sentence)

The number of times the executor cites an older fact when a newer fact should be the right answer - counted before and after enabling bi-temporal context - moves in opposite directions for two executors on the same workload.

Which executors and why

Two executor models were used for the closed-loop pilot runs: a more-capable model (a current-generation flagship from a major lab) and a smaller / cheaper model (a fast, lower-cost model from a different major lab). Both were available to the harness on the same interim provider path used for judging during this evaluation window; the canonical production path is EPAM DIAL (see Methods appendix). Two checkpoints is not sufficient to claim "capability tier is the only factor"; family bias remains possible. It is sufficient to motivate the structural claim: the value of extra context is conditional on the executor consuming it, not an absolute property of the substrate.

Specific checkpoints: Claude Sonnet 4.5 (the more-capable tier) and Gemini 2.5 Flash (the smaller / cheaper tier). The names are illustrative of the tier each was sampled at; the structural claim below is about the tier.

The flip

DIAGRAM · 07 · CONTEXT × CAPABILITY-TIER INTERACTION

The likely mechanism: the bi-temporal payload tells the executor "fact F1 was valid from time A to time B; fact F2 superseded it from time B". A smaller / cheaper executor reads that as "use F2, don't quote F1" and gets the supersession right. A more-capable executor reads the same payload, notices that F1 is still referenced in adjacent context, and occasionally re-quotes F1 - because the bi-temporal context surfaced its existence. The substrate did not become worse; the executor's reading of it changed.

What this finding does not say

It does not say more-capable executors are worse at memory. It does not say bi-temporal context is bad. It says: structural metadata interacts with executor capability in a way that vendor benchmarks do not surface, because vendor benchmarks rarely cross-multiply executor tier with substrate variant. Two checkpoints is not enough to rule out family bias; the conservative reading is "capability tier matters, family may also matter, the interaction is worth measuring per pair".

For platform teams setting defaults: ship bi-temporal context as a configurable behavior gated on capability tier, not as a global default. Default ON for smaller / cheaper executors; default OFF for more-capable executors; expose the toggle to the delivery team so they can re-evaluate per workload.

08 Adoption decision · Tencent DB Agent-Memory against the bar

Four-of-five fails. Decision reproducible against the bar.

The fourth candidate evaluated was Tencent DB Agent-Memory, the most architecturally-distinct option in the set - a four-layer local-memory pipeline with a Mermaid-based task-state working memory, published as an open-source project from the Tencent team. It is named here because the public state of the project is auditable and the rejection below is grounded in properties another reviewer can check against the same bar.

For delivery teams: name the bar before naming the candidate. A rejection that walks the criteria reads as decision discipline; a rejection that says "we did not like it" reads as taste.

The project promises a "fully local long-term memory pipeline with zero external API dependencies", which is a plausible fit for an SDLC-history workload at first glance. As of evaluation (mid-May 2026), no public third-party benchmark of the project exists - it is publicly unevaluated outside its own README. The evaluation below is, to public-source review, the first published external bar-check. Against the five-criterion bar in Section 03:

CriterionStatus on the candidateVerdict
Integration surface TypeScript / Node package targeted at one coding-agent framework. Consuming stack is Python-first. fail
Install hygiene Post-install step rewrites a sibling package's runtime files, silently falling through on error. fail
Test coverage Harness wired (runner + manifest commands), zero test files in the published source surface. fail
Maintenance posture ~6 weeks old at the most recent release; two contributors, one carrying the majority of commits. partial
Operational fit No MCP surface, no graph store, no fit with per-workspace HTTP or DIAL-routed model calls. fail

Four of five fails, one partial. The decision was clear and reproducible against the stated bar; another reviewer applying the same bar to the same project would reach the same conclusion. The honest reading: the project may mature into a serious option; against the bar today, it does not fit.

The honest framing

The four-of-five failure does not say the project is poorly designed. The four-layer pipeline idea is reasonable; the lack of an MCP surface is a deliberate choice given the framework it targets; the post-install patch is solving a real integration problem in its host framework. "Did not fit this adoption bar" is not the same as "is a bad project". The bar is calibrated to one specific delivery context; in another context the same project might clear the bar straightforwardly.

For delivery teams: revisit a rejected candidate when the bar changes (your stack adopts a new language, your platform team adopts a new gateway, the project ships a major release that addresses one of the failing criteria). The rejection is not permanent; the bar is the durable artefact.

09 Platform implications · DIAL-based delivery

What the findings imply for how memory is provisioned and routed.

The findings translate to four concrete defaults that the platform team should apply when standing up memory for the next DIAL-routed agent deployment, regardless of which account is delivering it.

  1. Chunk-text retrieval is the default substrate for SDLC-shaped corpora. The 36-point production-weighted gap is large enough that "use chunk-text by default; switch to entity-graph only with stated evidence" is the platform default. The narrow win for entity-graph on entity-disambiguation queries justifies a side path, not a switch of the default.
  2. Bi-temporal context is gated on capability tier. Default ON for smaller / cheaper executors, default OFF for more-capable executors, exposed as a configurable behavior to the delivery team. The cost of getting this wrong is silent: a more-capable executor with bi-temporal context re-quotes the wrong fact in a way that is silently wrong until a read pass catches it.
  3. Autonomous-writeback paths use the substrate with no extraction step on read. Until the failure mode of an extraction-mediated substrate is bounded, the substrate whose failure mode is "the wrong chunk" is the one that gets autonomous writeback. A review-gated assist path can use either substrate.
  4. Model calls should route through the EPAM DIAL gateway in production. This evaluation's judging and substrate stack used an interim provider path while DIAL provisioning completed; re-run retrieval and judging on DIAL before citing the numbers as deployment-transferable for DIAL-routed agents.

For platform teams: encode these four as defaults in the per-workspace memory-substrate configuration. The configuration surface should make the right choice the easy choice, and the wrong choice an explicit opt-out.

10 Recommendations · For EPAM delivery teams

What to do this quarter, what to do this year, what to leave alone.

The findings translate to three time-horizons of action for delivery teams choosing memory for the next enterprise-agent engagement.

This quarter (immediate)

  • Identify which enterprise class your customer's corpus is in (SDLC-history or business / domain). The class determines the default substrate; choosing the wrong class is the most common source of underperformance later.
  • Run a 20- to 60-question canary on the actual corpus before adoption. The canary should cover at least the two dominant query families for the corpus's class. Position-swap judging, two judges from different model families, on the inference path you will use in production (if you measured on an interim path, budget a DIAL-canonical re-run before go-live).
  • Write your adoption bar before you start evaluating candidates, calibrated to the customer's stack. Use Section 03's five criteria as a starting point and add domain-specific criteria.

This year (medium-term)

  • Build a canary harness that an account team can re-run on a new corpus in a day rather than a week. The harness is the durable asset; specific findings expire when models or substrates ship new versions.
  • Cross-tier the canary across at least two executor capability tiers per substrate. The tier-interaction finding (Section 07) is invisible from single-tier evaluation; missing it is how a substrate that looked safe in evaluation surprises in production.
  • Establish a default-rejection profile against the adoption bar. Most candidates fail on integration surface or install hygiene before any retrieval-quality evaluation is needed. Filter cheap before you measure expensive.

What to leave alone

  • The vendor's published benchmark numbers, as a deployment input. They are useful for vendor comparison; they are not the deployment decision. The deployment decision is your canary on your corpus.
  • The single-substrate-as-universal-default reflex. Pair the substrate to the dominant query family, not to a vendor preference. Most production workloads benefit from two substrate paths (chunk-text default, entity-graph side path) rather than one.

11 Scope limits · What this report does not claim

The contract between the report and the next reader who acts on it.

Each of the findings has a scope. Stating the scope as plainly as the finding is what keeps the report from being over-read into contexts where the magnitudes do not transfer.

  1. One corpus class, one work instance. The empirical work was carried out on an SDLC-history corpus of around 130 documents with the production query distribution stated in Section 04. The pattern (run your own canary, weight by your production distribution, measure cross-tier) transfers across corpora and classes. The specific 36-percentage-point gap, the 0-vs-4 fabrication count, and the stale-fact inversion magnitude do not transfer without re-measurement.
  2. Small N per family. Eight paired questions per task family detects large effects per family. Wherever the per-family table shows a tie at this sample size, the two substrates may or may not be equivalent - the canary cannot tell at this N. Higher-N follow-ups are in scope for a re-run.
  3. The filesystem baseline is intentionally naïve. One-shot keyword extraction over the corpus, head of each matched file to the judge. A retrieval-quality-matched filesystem baseline (embedding-based reranker, iterative re-query) would close part of the gap. The naïve baseline reflects what a team does when no purpose-built substrate is available, which is the right comparison for "is Tier-3 worth keeping" - not "is your filesystem optimal".
  4. Two-judge consensus inflates ties. Position-swap collapse discards information when the two orderings disagree. This is the conservative inferential layer (it rules out position bias) but it discards substrate-quality signal on the agreed-tie pool. A taste-of-the-tie-pool secondary read is a follow-up.
  5. Provider invariance is assumed at temperature 0. The bench harness routed model calls through an interim provider path during the evaluation window; a DIAL-canonical re-run is in scope for the next iteration. The assumption - that at temperature 0 the same model returns the same answer regardless of routing - is plausible and not separately measured here.
  6. The tier-interaction finding rests on two executor checkpoints. Two checkpoints from two model families is not sufficient to fully separate capability-tier effects from family-specific effects. The conservative reading is "capability tier matters, family may also matter, the interaction is worth measuring per pair". Adding more checkpoints within each family is in scope for a re-run.
  7. The adoption-bar evaluation is project-state-as-of-evaluation-time. A project that fails the bar today may clear it after a major release. The bar is the durable artefact; the rejection is re-evaluable when the candidate ships material changes against the failing criteria.
  8. Cost per decision is not in this report. Substrate ingest costs (per-document embedding, per-extraction LLM calls) and substrate read costs (per-query model calls) differ by roughly an order of magnitude between the two approaches. The end-to-end cost-per-decision calculation belongs in a separate study tied to a deployed workload.
  9. Closed-loop counts are pilot-level. The stale-fact inversion per-cell numbers in Section 07 are from a closed-loop pilot (eight tasks per cell), not the formal 8-task closed-loop suite, which remains deferred. Citing those counts as measured benchmark results is out of scope until the formal suite ships.

12 Closing · The take-away

The substrate decision is downstream of the measurement.

The headline numbers in this report do not generalise. The discipline that produced them does. A deployment decision that survives the first production query is one that was settled by measurement on the production workload, not by adoption of a vendor's headline number.

The artefact worth taking from this report is the canary harness shape (paired questions, position-swap judging, two judges from different model families, on the gateway you will deploy through - this report measured on an interim path; DIAL-canonical re-run pending), the adoption bar (five named criteria applied uniformly), and the section arc of a deployment-decision memo (pattern first, findings as evidence, methods last). Re-instantiate each against the corpus and the executor you are about to deploy on, and the substrate decision falls out.

The closing thesis

Applied AI is the discipline that converts frontier research and consumer-grade features into enterprise systems with measured properties, named failure modes, and a stated adoption bar.

Frontier labs publish capability. Consumer products dramatise it. Neither layer ships the tenancy, audit, schema-stability, cost-bound, and recovery contract that an enterprise substrate has to honour. The discipline that closes that gap is what Applied AI names: the work of taking a specific frontier-or-consumer capability, choosing an adoption bar, running a canary on the actual production workload, and writing the result up so the next team can re-instantiate it. The substrate decision below is one specific instance of that work, on long-term memory for coding-orchestration agents. The pattern is portable across other capabilities the same enterprises will keep encountering: agent autonomy, cost-per-decision observability, multi-tenant inference, and the next frontier result that lands without an enterprise deployment story attached.

Judging rubric

Each retrieval was labelled by both judges as one of: correct (complete and accurate), partial (relevant but incomplete), incorrect (a real source, but the wrong one), abstained (substrate returned nothing usable), or hallucinated (substrate returned content that does not exist in any source document). Both judges saw both orderings of each pair; a pair "won" only when the same candidate was preferred in both orderings by both judges. Position-swap disagreement collapsed to tie.

Statistical bar

The rule-change threshold was pre-registered before any retrieval ran: a result clears the bar when the absolute win-rate gap is ≥20 percentage points, when the fabrication-rate ratio is ≥2×, or when the 95% bootstrap-bias-corrected-accelerated confidence interval excludes zero. McNemar's exact test on discordant pairs and a Beta-Binomial Bayesian posterior were computed as secondary reads; neither was used as the rule-change trigger on its own.

Frequency weighting

The production-weighted aggregate used the eight-family weights stated in Section 04. The weights were observed on the actual production workload over a representative interval; they are not the equal weights a uniform aggregate would assume. The weighting itself was applied to per-family win-rates after the position-swap collapse, so the same conservative inferential layer governed both aggregates.
