Picking the extraction LLM for knowledge-graph ingestion.

Knowledge-graph RAG systems (Microsoft GraphRAG, LightRAG, the ecosystem around them) extract entities and relations from source text at ingest time using an LLM. Published benchmarks for these systems compare retrieval quality across graph backends and embedders. They hold the extraction LLM constant. In production it is the variable that dominates both cost and retrieval quality, and the published numbers do not name a default. I ran eleven candidate models on one such system (LightRAG, used here as the representative implementation), same corpus, same chunking, same smoke rubric. One model passed every retrieval cell at roughly three percent of the strongest commercial alternative's ingest cost. The counter-intuitive part is which axis decides it: breadth of extraction, not density of relations.

01 Executive summary

Three findings, one model recommendation.

The companion memory substrate report answers the question "which graph substrate." This report answers the question that operationally dominates that one for any knowledge-graph RAG system: which LLM should the substrate use during ingestion. The choice is not interchangeable. It controls retrieval quality more than the substrate choice does, it controls cost by an order of magnitude or more, and a single misconfigured workstation file can flip a healthy substrate to red overnight. The measurements below are on LightRAG specifically (it is the canonical open-source implementation of this class), but the findings are about the class. I ran eleven candidates against a fixed 20-document subset and a 24-cell smoke rubric. One model passed every cell. I validated it on a separate 482-document legal corpus before recommending it for production.

Finding 1. Breadth beats density for graph-traversal retrieval

Gemini 2.5 Flash extracts roughly 2x more entities and 1.6x more relations per document than Claude Sonnet 4.6 on the same chunks. Its relation-to-entity ratio is actually slightly lower (1.15 vs 1.43 on the smoke corpus). The mechanism: graph-traversal retrieval (the shape every system in this class uses) fetches chunks anchored by the entity index, so additional entities anchor additional chunks at retrieval time. Density of relations matters less than the surface area the entity index covers. Sonnet 4.6 scored 22/0/2 on the smoke rubric (two fail cells, zero marginals); Gemini 2.5 Flash scored a clean 24/0/0. The breadth ranking holds across every corpus I have measured; the absolute entity-per-document numbers vary by roughly 2x across them (see §05). claims-ledger →

Finding 2. The cost-quality Pareto has one sweet spot, not a curve

OpenAI gpt-4o-mini ingests at roughly one-fifth to one-third of Gemini 2.5 Flash's list-priced cost but loses smoke cells on cross-file synthesis (19 pass, 3 marginal, 2 fail). Sonnet 4.6 ingests at roughly thirty-three times Gemini Flash's measured cost and scores 22/24 (two fail cells). There is no smooth cost-quality frontier in the measured matrix; there is one model that pays for itself by clearing all 24 cells at the lowest cost that clears all 24. Two later Gemini variants (3-flash-preview, 3.1-flash-lite-preview) extracted more conservatively and lost cells; "newer" is not "better" for this task. cost-model →

Finding 3. Local open-source ingestion is hardware-bound, not capability-bound

Six open-source local trials (Llama 3.2 3B, Qwen 3.5 4B, Gemma 4 26B, Qwen 2.5 14B in two quantizations, Qwen 2.5 32B) showed a single recurring failure pattern: small models cannot reliably emit LightRAG's pipe-delimited entity schema, mid-size models thrash on consumer-class GPUs, and 14B-class Qwen models on M1-Max hardware finish the corpus but lose cells to schema noise. Qwen 2.5 14B GGUF Q4 was the best local result and still scored 5/4/15 on the same rubric where Gemini Flash scored 24/0/0. Local makes sense for sensitivity reasons, not for cost or quality reasons.

02 What ingest actually controls

The extraction LLM is the substrate.

Knowledge-graph RAG systems all share the same ingest shape. The published benchmarks for these systems hold the extractor constant, the architecture diagrams hide it inside the ingest box, the ablation tables vary chunk size and retrieval mode but not extractor. In production it is the variable that decides what your graph looks like.

The class of system this report studies includes Microsoft GraphRAG, LightRAG, and the smaller ecosystem of similar pipelines built on Neo4j vector-plus-graph storage. They differ in retrieval strategy, in graph schema, in how they expose configuration. They share one ingest pattern: for each chunk of source text, an LLM extracts entities, an LLM extracts relations among them, an LLM summarises the entity when it appears in many chunks, an LLM gleans a second pass to recover missed extractions. All four are LLM calls. The LLM call dominates wall-clock time, dominates dollar cost, and produces every node and edge in the graph. The graph is what retrieval traverses. So the extractor's biases (which entity types it recognises, which relation types it emits, how strictly it honours the schema) become permanent properties of the substrate until you re-ingest from scratch.

The published benchmarks for these systems rarely specify or vary the extractor. LightRAG's paper names gpt-4o-mini for some runs and reports retrieval scores; it does not study sensitivity to extractor choice. A reader who deploys the system with the upstream defaults gets gpt-4o-mini as the implicit extractor and inherits its limits without ever seeing the choice. That implicit-default-as-decision pattern is the failure mode this report tries to make explicit, on LightRAG as the representative implementation.

Three properties of the extractor that show up downstream:

  • Entity count per document. Determines how much of the chunk text is reachable from any given query. More entities means more anchor points, means more chunks retrievable when a query lands on any of them.
  • Schema fidelity. LightRAG expects pipe-delimited records (<entity|description|type|...>). Models that violate the format have their output dropped by the parser. Schema noise looks like missing data in the graph but is actually rejected extraction.
  • Description quality. When LightRAG asks the LLM to summarise an entity that appears in many chunks, the summary becomes the canonical description stored in the graph. A weak summary degrades query-time matching even when the entity itself was extracted correctly.

None of these is what a chat-quality benchmark would measure. The extractor's job is structured-text production under a tight schema with no narrative. The best chat model on Arena leaderboards is not necessarily the best extractor.

03 Method

The rubric is fixed before any model runs.

Stating the rubric a priori is what makes a benchmark reproducible. Stating it post hoc reduces to a justification of whatever happened. Five things held constant across all eleven model rows:

Corpus. 20 documents from a personal knowledge-base corpus, pinned by explicit file list (not by directory glob) so the same files are ingested for every model row. Average ~5 pages each, mixed prose and structured notes. The pinning discipline matters: a benchmark that uses "the first 20 files alphabetically" silently drifts as new files land. A separate 482-document legal corpus was used for production-class validation of the winning model; that corpus produced different absolute entity counts on the same model and chunking, which is reported honestly in §05.

Chunking. LightRAG defaults: 1200 tokens per chunk, 100 token overlap. Approximately 4 chunks per document on this corpus. Per chunk, the ingest pipeline runs entity extraction plus relation extraction plus an occasional merge-summary call, totalling roughly 2.5 LLM calls per chunk on average.

Embedder. text-embedding-mxbai-embed-large-v1 served by LM Studio on M1 Max, 1024-dimensional, local. Held constant across all model rows so that the variable under study is the extractor only.

Smoke rubric. Six canary queries times four retrieval modes (naive, local, global, hybrid) equals 24 cells per model. Each cell is scored pass / marginal / fail by the same evaluator looking at the returned chunks against the documented expected references. The cells and the expected references were written before any model ran. The rubric protocol, schema, and an example fixture on a neutral domain are published as the smoke-rubric artefact so a reader can re-instantiate the matrix on their own corpus.

Reporting. Each row records entities per document, relations per document, relation-to-entity ratio, smoke score as pass/marginal/fail, and measured cost on the 20-document subset. Cost is measured at the model provider's invoice level (OpenRouter or local zero-cost), not estimated.

What this method does NOT measure

It does not measure end-to-end retrieval quality on the full corpus class. The 24-cell smoke rubric is a fast acceptance gate, not a full evaluation harness. Models that pass it earn a production trial on the larger 482-document corpus; that trial is where the production claims live. It also does not measure latency or throughput at scale, which would dominate the picture for streaming-ingest workloads (this report is for batch ingest, not streaming).

The rubric was scored by a single evaluator (me) without an inter-rater check or an LLM-as-judge second pass. The 264 cells (11 models x 24 cells) sit inside one person's judgement of what counts as `pass` versus `marginal`. The schema in the smoke-rubric artefact includes the inter-rater discipline that would close this gap; the operator's matrix did not run it. A second-scorer pass on this matrix is the single highest-value re-measurement another practitioner could contribute back.

05 Breadth vs density

Why the lower ratio wins.

The counter-intuitive finding sits in two columns of the matrix. Gemini 2.5 Flash produces a graph with 2x the entities and 1.6x the relations per document compared to Claude Sonnet 4.6, but its relation-to-entity ratio is lower (1.15 vs 1.43). On a chat benchmark you would expect the higher-ratio extractor to win on synthesis tasks because each entity is more richly connected. On this benchmark the opposite happens. The model that produces more loosely connected nodes wins.

The mechanism is in the retrieval shape shared by every system in this class. A query is embedded and matched against entity nodes; the top-k entity neighbourhoods are traversed; the chunks that originally produced those entities are fetched and returned. Whether the right chunk gets returned depends primarily on whether any entity in that chunk matches the query, not on how densely entities link to each other. Doubling the entity count roughly doubles the surface area the entity index covers, which roughly doubles the probability that a query lands on at least one entity from each chunk that should be returned.

Relation density matters too, but only on a smaller class of queries: those that require multi-hop traversal across the graph (e.g. "what links concept A to concept B in this corpus"). Most production queries do not look like that. They look like "what does this corpus say about X." For those, breadth of the entity index dominates density of the relation index.

This pattern generalises across knowledge-graph RAG systems with traversal-then-fetch retrieval: optimise the extractor for entity coverage first, and only optimise for relation density when the query distribution actually rewards it. It does not generalise to systems that score retrieval purely on relation paths (which is a smaller class than the literature implies), or to pure vector RAG without an entity index.

Honest scope: the breadth ratio holds; the absolute counts do not

The 82.5 entities per document for Gemini 2.5 Flash is the number from the 20-document smoke corpus. Same model, same chunking, on two other production-shaped corpora I run: a 482-document legal corpus produced 39.8 entities per document (and a higher rel/ent ratio of 1.43), and a recent partial re-ingest of a 17-document working corpus produced 59.9 entities per document. The model's breadth advantage over Sonnet 4.6 holds on all three; the absolute breadth varies by roughly a factor of two depending on what is in the chunks. The recommendation in §10 (default to Gemini 2.5 Flash, re-validate the smoke rubric on your own corpus) is what falls out of this: the ranking transfers, the magnitudes do not.

06 Per-100-doc cost

Normalised so you can project your own corpus.

The five rows of the cost table below were measured on the same 20-document corpus, with the per-100-document figures derived as documented in the cost-model artefact. I do not name a total dollar figure because the absolute number depends on document length, chunking, gleaning aggressiveness, and the contract price with whatever gateway sits in front of the model. The per-100-document numbers let you multiply by your own corpus size; for two enterprise-scale worked examples, see the cost-model artefact's "enterprise-scale worked examples" section.

The full derivation is in the cost-model artefact. Each document on this corpus produced approximately 4 chunks of 1200 tokens each, and LightRAG made approximately 2.5 LLM calls per chunk on average (entity extraction + relation extraction + occasional merge-summary), with each call consuming roughly 8K input tokens and producing roughly 2K output tokens. That comes to approximately 80K input + 20K output tokens per document, or 8M input + 2M output tokens per 100 documents, before any gleaning overhead.

One reconciliation note: measured invoices on the smoke corpus can run above the list-price calculator (~$7 to $10 per 100 documents at the rates above) because gleaning passes fire more aggressively in real ingest runs than the chunk-token math predicts, and because the model adds explanatory text the cost-calculator does not anticipate. The cost-model artefact walks through both methods. Use the invoice-based number when projecting cost-of-mistake (the drift case in §09), the calculator number when negotiating contract pricing.

Why list-price math hides the real bottleneck

The per-token rate is not the binding constraint on a full re-ingest at scale. The binding constraint is the daily token quota of the account tier you are on. A 180-document re-ingest in flight on a developer-tier Gemini key tripped a 2,000,000-token daily cap and parked the remaining documents until the quota reset. The list-price column says ingest is cheap. The capacity column says ingest is slow on the wrong tier.

07 Open-source local trials

Hardware is the binding constraint.

The local-extractor question matters separately from cost. Some corpora cannot leave the operator's machine for sensitivity reasons. For those, the question is not "is local cheaper" (cloud is so cheap on this workload that the cost argument barely survives) but "is local viable at all."

I ran six local trials across two hardware tiers. Consumer GPU (a GTX 1070 with 6 GB VRAM serving Ollama) handled Llama 3.2 3B Q4 and Qwen 3.5 4B but timed out on Gemma 4 26B and 502-cascaded on Qwen 2.5 32B. M1 Max with 32 GB unified memory ran Qwen 2.5 14B in two flavours: MLX 4-bit cascaded on the embed call, GGUF Q4_K_M finished the corpus over ~4 hours and earned 5/4/15 on the smoke rubric.

Four transferable observations:

  • Schema noise dominates small-model output. The 3B Llama row produced hundreds of schema-violation warnings on the 20-document corpus. LightRAG's parser dropped most of the model's extraction output. The model "succeeded" in the sense that ingest completed; the graph it produced was sparse because most of its work was rejected. This is invisible without explicit schema-violation counting and easy to miss in a quick local trial.
  • Concurrency mismatch between LightRAG and the local inference server is its own failure mode. The MLX runs of Qwen 2.5 14B aborted on what looked like extraction failures and was actually a cascade: LightRAG's EMBEDDING_FUNC_MAX_ASYNC defaults to 8, LM Studio's Parallel setting defaults to 4, and the eight in-flight embedding requests overran the four worker slots and timed out, which the LightRAG side then retried and amplified. The fix is to either align the two (cap LightRAG at 4) or run the embedder on a separate host with its own concurrency budget. This is the kind of diagnostic that does not show up in any one component's logs because the failure is in the seam between them.
  • Thinking-mode models burn the worker timeout. Reasoning-style models (Gemma 4 26B with default thinking enabled, Qwen 3 series with default thinking) emit a long internal monologue before the structured output. On a per-chunk LightRAG call this consistently pushes wall-clock past the worker timeout. Where a no-think variant exists, default to it for structured extraction; thinking-mode is a chat-quality feature that costs you ingest viability.
  • Mid-size on M1 is the local sweet spot. Qwen 2.5 14B GGUF Q4_K_M was the best local result and is the model I would deploy if a corpus had to stay local. It still loses two-thirds of the smoke rubric to Gemini Flash. Local is a viability choice driven by data residency, not a quality choice.

08 No-think discipline

Reasoning modes are a tax on extraction.

Reasoning mode is a chat feature. On a structured-extraction workload it is a tax. The thinking-mode failure pattern from §07 is general enough to deserve its own principle: reasoning-style models that emit a chain-of-thought before the structured output produce two undesirable effects during LightRAG ingest:

  1. Per-call latency goes up by the length of the thought. A non-thinking extraction call takes seconds; the same call with reasoning enabled routinely takes several times longer because the model emits a chain-of-thought the parser then discards. Exact multipliers depend on the model family; the consistent observation across the families I tested was "long enough to blow out the worker timeout on a default LightRAG config." The pipeline does not get more accurate output; it gets the same structured record with a long preamble.
  2. The thought tokens are billable. On commercial providers, reasoning tokens count toward output cost. A reasoning-mode extraction call can cost several times what a non-reasoning call costs for the same final output, again depending on the model.

The recommendation: when a model family offers a no-think variant or a flag to disable reasoning, default to it for structured-extraction workloads. Verify the flag actually disables reasoning end-to-end. Some Ollama versions silently ignore think: false on the OpenAI-compatible /v1/chat/completions endpoint and honour it only on the native /api/chat endpoint. That kind of plumbing detail is the difference between a model that ingests in two hours and a model that ingests in twelve.

The principle generalises beyond LightRAG. Any pipeline that uses an LLM as a structured-output generator under a tight schema should disable reasoning by default, because the reasoning tokens add cost and latency without adding schema-compliant output.

09 The drift incident · postscript, 2026-05-20

One wrong line in one workstation file.

Postscript added 2026-05-20. The benchmark above was finalised on 2026-04-28 and Gemini 2.5 Flash was locked in as the production extractor. Three weeks later, ingestion on my primary workspace had silently drifted back to Claude Sonnet 4.6, and a re-ingest at roughly thirty times the expected cost was flagged red by the canary. I am adding this section to the original report rather than publishing it separately because the failure pattern is structural to the decision the report describes, not a one-off mistake, and a reader acting on the recommendations deserves the full story.

The chain of events:

  1. A separate ingest job had run out of a personal-tier daily token quota mid-corpus, leaving most of a vault's worth of documents in failed state.
  2. A recovery attempt edited the workstation's launchd plist to use Sonnet 4.6, citing it as "the proven config" because that was the config for a different production workspace.
  3. The wrong model name persisted through subsequent infrastructure work; later edits preserved it implicitly by not re-checking the per-workspace correct answer.
  4. A re-ingest at Sonnet 4.6 rates ran to substantial completion before the canary detected that retrieval was failing.
  5. The canary instrumentation that caught the drift had itself been merged that week. Before it existed, an empty substrate had been showing green.

Three lessons, each with a concrete instrumentation answer:

  • Per-workspace extractor choice does not generalise. "The proven config" is not a fact about the company or the gateway; it is a fact about one specific workspace. Copying it to another workspace without re-validating against that workspace's corpus is the same class of error as copying a database index from one table to another because it worked on the first. The instrumentation answer: render the per-workspace plist from a template that takes the workspace name and the chosen extractor as parameters, so a copy-paste cannot omit the per-workspace step.
  • An empty substrate looks the same as a healthy one to the wrong canary. The canary that catches drift has to assert what a working result looks like (specific references returned, specific entity types present), not just that the substrate responded without error. A "no relevant context found" response is a valid response from an empty graph and from a healthy graph that the query missed; without anchored assertions the gate cannot distinguish. The instrumentation answer: anchor the canary to specific expected references and fail the gate when the response does not name them. The week's foundation-gate change that caught the drift did exactly this.
  • Configuration drift between repo and host is invisible until it produces a bill. The plist on the workstation had a hand-edited LLM_MODEL value that was not in the repo template. Subsequent edits saw "this file is already configured" and preserved the wrong value. The instrumentation answer: save every plist edit alongside a model-tagged backup (the workstation now has files like ai.lightrag.<workspace>.plist.bak.<model>-<date>) so the deployed state is always one diff away from any prior intent, and run a check that fails when the deployed plist diverges from the rendered template.

10 Recommendations

What to deploy, what to instrument.

The substrate question and the extractor question are answered together. For a coding-orchestration corpus or a knowledge-base corpus shaped like the one I measured, running on any knowledge-graph RAG system in this class:

  • Default extractor: Gemini 2.5 Flash via the provider gateway you already use. Re-validate the smoke rubric on a 20-document subset of your own corpus before locking it in. The rubric file is small enough to put in your repo.
  • Reject the implicit gpt-4o-mini default. Several systems in this class ship gpt-4o-mini or an equivalent small-tier model as the implicit extractor. It is roughly three to five times cheaper than Gemini Flash at list price and loses cells you will care about. If you cannot upgrade the extractor, at least make the choice explicit so future operators see it.
  • Treat the relation-to-entity ratio as diagnostic, not optimisation target. A ratio above ~1.4 with entity count below ~30 per document indicates a dense small graph that will underperform retrieval at scale. A ratio near 1.0 with entity count above ~60 indicates a wide sparse graph that traverses well.
  • For local-only corpora: Qwen 2.5 14B GGUF Q4_K_M in LM Studio on Apple Silicon (32 GB or more). Pair it with a separate query-time model for synthesis. Accept that you are trading retrieval breadth for data residency.
  • Disable reasoning mode on any extractor that supports it. Verify the flag actually reaches the model.
  • Budget for daily token caps before they bite. Even cheap extractors hit soft daily caps during a full vault re-ingest. Plan for the higher pricing tier or stagger the re-ingest across days; the cost difference is small versus losing a working substrate for a day.
  • Run one extractor process per workspace. Multi-tenant LightRAG sounds tidy until two workspaces want different extractors. Per-workspace processes (one binary per workspace, the registry enforces it) keep the LLM cache from cross-contaminating extractions and make per-workspace model swaps a one-line config change instead of a full multi-tenant migration.

And three operational instrumentation recommendations forced by the drift incident:

  • Anchor the ingest-health canary to expected entities. "Does this query return at least one chunk that mentions Y" not "does the substrate respond." Implement it as a gate that fails on the substring-anywhere variant of the model's empty-response phrase, and on the absence of the expected reference, both.
  • Render deployed extractor config from a templated source. A small renderer that takes (workspace name, extractor, embedder) and emits the plist or systemd unit makes the per-workspace step structural, not a copy-paste. Diff the deployed file against the rendered output on every cron tick; alarm on divergence.
  • Keep model-tagged backups of every config edit. When the deployed config changes, save the previous version with a model-and-date suffix. The cost is bytes; the value is that the next operator can see what was tried and what it cost.

If you are doing this for more than one team

The recommendations above are for an individual operator running one to a handful of workspaces. An enterprise team adding this to a sanctioned-inference platform inherits three more concerns:

  • Per-workspace billing tagging. The per-process model from §10 makes per-workspace cost attribution straightforward (one process, one billing tag, one line item). Multi-tenant LightRAG with shared LLM cache makes this nearly impossible. Pick the deployment model with billing in mind, not after.
  • Workspace provisioning workflow. The templated renderer that catches drift in §09 also becomes the per-team self-serve workspace provisioner. A team requests a workspace, the renderer emits the plist or systemd unit, the canary is registered, the workspace is live. If your platform team is not the bottleneck for new-workspace creation, this scales; otherwise it does not.
  • Vendor lock-in budgeted as re-ingest cost. Gemini 2.5 Flash will eventually be deprecated or repriced. The unit of swap is one full re-ingest at the new model's rate (cost from §06), the gate is the smoke rubric on the new model, and the rollout is a config change rather than a code change because of the renderer. Audit committees that ask "what is your exit plan" can be answered with the smoke-rubric file plus a re-ingest cost projection. Most enterprise-AI procurement does not ask this question yet; it will.

11 Scope

Where these numbers do not transfer.

Every report's section eleven lists the conditions under which the magnitudes do not transfer. Each scope claim has a corresponding falsification condition in the invalidation artefact.

  • Corpus shape. The 20-document smoke corpus and 482-document validation corpus are both mixed-prose Markdown notes with implicit entity types (people, projects, decisions, dates). A corpus dominated by structured data (database schemas, code), pure narrative (novels, transcripts), or specialist vocabularies (medical, legal) may produce a different model ranking. The smoke rubric is reproducible on your corpus; rerun it before generalising.
  • Absolute breadth varies by corpus. Gemini 2.5 Flash extracted 82.5 entities per document on the 20-doc smoke corpus, 39.8 on a 482-document legal corpus, and 59.9 on a 17-document working corpus today. The ranking against other models held in every case; the absolute counts varied by roughly a factor of two. Do not project specific magnitudes from this report onto your corpus; re-measure on the corpus that matters to you.
  • Chunking. All rows used LightRAG defaults (1200 tokens, 100 overlap). A different chunk size changes per-chunk LLM cost and the entity-to-chunk anchoring shape. Re-derive the cost table if you change chunking.
  • Embedder. All rows used the same local embedder. Changing the embedder changes retrieval behaviour independently of the extractor and would warrant a separate study.
  • Provider pricing. List prices used here are public rate cards. Contract pricing through an enterprise gateway will shift absolute magnitudes; the cost ratios inside the matrix are more stable than the absolutes.
  • Query distribution. Breadth-over-density holds for traversal-then-fetch retrieval (the shape LightRAG, Microsoft GraphRAG, and similar systems use). Systems that score retrieval on multi-hop relation paths may reward density more. Pure vector RAG without an entity index does not benefit from this finding at all.
  • Implementation tested. Measurements were taken on LightRAG. The findings about extraction-LLM choice should generalise to other systems in the same class (Microsoft GraphRAG, Neo4j vector-plus-graph setups), but the absolute entity counts and cost numbers depend on the specific pipeline's prompt templates and gleaning aggressiveness. Re-derive on your chosen implementation before treating the magnitudes as portable.
  • Model versions. Frontier models update frequently. Gemini 2.5 Flash specifically beat its own 3.x successors on this task; do not assume a newer release will preserve the same extraction behaviour.

12 Closing

The extractor is not an implementation detail.

The thing I want a delivery team to take from this report is not the specific model. Gemini 2.5 Flash will be obsolete by the next time someone reads this. The thing to take is the discipline: when the substrate's published benchmarks hold a variable constant, that variable is usually the one that decides outcomes in production. Name it, run a smoke rubric on it before locking it in, and instrument for the drift case where someone replaces it for the wrong reason.

The accidental Sonnet ingest cost between roughly ten times and roughly thirty times the right number (depending on whether you count list-priced calculator math or invoice-measured spend on this specific corpus) and produced a graph that retrieval failed on. The benchmark that named the right model existed in the vault for three weeks before the drift happened. The recovery took less than a day once the right diagnostic existed. The expensive lesson was not "Sonnet is the wrong extractor" (which was already documented), it was "the bridge between a documented decision and a deployed config has to be instrumented or the decision does not survive contact with operations."

The full apparatus is in artefacts/: the methodology, the claims ledger, the falsification conditions, the model matrix, the cost-model derivation. Re-instantiate the smoke rubric on your own corpus before committing to a default. The model that wins on yours may not be the model that wins on mine.
